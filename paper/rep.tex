A Systematic Study of Dense Reward Geometry\\
for Robot Manipulation}

\begin{abstract}
Dense reward design for robot manipulation is a persistent bottleneck: small changes
in reward formulas can produce qualitatively different learning outcomes, yet the field
lacks principled guidance for making these choices. We present a systematic empirical
study of dense reward geometry using ManiSkill3's tabletop manipulation suite as a
controlled testbed, injecting reward variants as environment parameters and holding
algorithms and tasks fixed to isolate causal effects. Our experiments establish two
core findings. First, a fundamental \emph{robustness-ceiling tradeoff}: infinite-support
rewards (e.g.\ \texttt{tanh}) trap policies in \emph{hover attractors}---stable
sub-optimal states where the shaped reward is high but task completion is not---while
finite-support rewards (\texttt{concave\_truncated}) resist this but create blind
regions for hard initialisations. Within-episode annealing resolves the tradeoff on
flat tasks (PickCube: $0.979$, PushCube: $0.958$, LiftPegUpright: $0.854$) but
catastrophically fails on hierarchical tasks (StackCube: mean peak $0.063$ vs.\
$1.000$ for finite-support)---a complete ranking inversion caused by the time clock
decoupling from task progress at stage boundaries. Second, this failure motivates a
\emph{theoretically grounded} alternative: \texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance}, a
closed-form static reward in which the support radius self-adapts to the agent's current
distance from the goal via $k(d) = k_{\min} + (k_{\max}-k_{\min})e^{-\alpha d}$.
We derive conditions on $k_{\min}$, $k_{\max}$, $\alpha$ governing the reward's
geometry, and show empirically that it matches \texttt{concave\_truncated}'s peak
performance on StackCube (mean peak $0.979$ vs.\ $1.000$) while converging
approximately $2\times$ faster. The result is a \emph{reward engineering playbook}
with a theoretically principled base function for both flat and hierarchical tasks.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{reward shaping, dense rewards, robot manipulation, reinforcement learning,
reward design, empirical study}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reinforcement learning for robot manipulation requires dense reward functions to guide
exploration: purely sparse rewards that fire only at success are rarely sufficient for
contact-rich, multi-stage tasks. Yet practitioners designing these dense rewards have
few principled guidelines. The standard approach is to write a distance-based potential
that decreases toward the goal, add binary stage bonuses, and tune scale factors
empirically until training converges. This process is slow, non-transferable, and yields
little understanding of which mathematical properties of the reward function matter.

The question we study is: \emph{given that the task and algorithm are fixed, how does
the geometry of the dense reward function---its shape, curvature, support, smoothness,
and staging---causally affect learning dynamics and final performance?}

This is distinct from several related lines of work. Automated reward generation
methods~\citep{ma2024eureka} improve the speed of reward iteration but treat reward code
as a black box to be searched rather than a structured object to be understood.
Reward shaping theory~\citep{ng1999shaping,devlin2012potential} provides guarantees
about policy invariance under specific transformations but says little about optimization
speed or empirical ranking among non-equivalent reward functions. What is missing is a
controlled, systematic empirical study of reward geometry under on-policy learning in
manipulation.

We address this gap by decomposing dense manipulation rewards into four independently
parameterizable dimensions (\S\ref{sec:taxonomy}), implementing them as environment
kwargs in ManiSkill3 (\S\ref{sec:setup}), and sweeping across combinations while
holding everything else fixed.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item A taxonomy of dense reward geometry along four independently controllable
    axes, implemented as environment kwargs across 11 ManiSkill3 tabletop tasks
    (Appendix~\ref{app:tasks}).
    \item Identification and characterisation of the \emph{hover attractor}---a
    failure mode of infinite-support rewards---and the fundamental
    \emph{robustness-ceiling tradeoff} between hover attractors and blind regions.
    \item A mechanistic account of why within-episode time annealing succeeds on
    flat tasks but catastrophically fails on hierarchical ones (complete ranking
    inversion on StackCube).
    \item A first-principles theory of distance-adaptive reward geometry
    (Section~\ref{sec:theory}), deriving closed-form conditions on the
    reward's support structure, moat existence, and parameter roles.
    \item \texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance}: a principled static reward derived
    from the theory that achieves comparable peak performance to the best
    task-specific static reward while converging $\approx\!2\times$ faster on
    StackCube-v1 (mean peak $0.979$).
    \item A reward engineering playbook: empirically grounded, theoretically
    motivated guidance for dense reward selection by task structure.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Reward shaping theory.}
\citet{ng1999shaping} established that potential-based shaping does not alter the
optimal policy in the limit, but this result does not address optimization dynamics
under finite-sample on-policy methods. \citet{devlin2012potential} extended the theory
to dynamic potential functions. Our work is empirical and focuses on how shaping
geometry affects convergence speed and stability rather than asymptotic correctness.

\paragraph{Automated reward design.}
EUREKA~\citep{ma2024eureka} uses LLMs to generate reward code and refine it via
evolutionary search, achieving impressive results across locomotion and manipulation
tasks. However, the method treats reward formulas as programs to be searched, not as
geometric objects to be understood. Our work is complementary: we aim to understand
\emph{why} certain reward structures work, which could inform better search spaces for
automated methods.

\paragraph{Empirical studies of RL design choices.}
\citet{andrychowicz2020matters} conducted a large-scale study of on-policy RL design
choices (architecture, normalization, etc.) but did not focus on reward geometry.
To our knowledge, no comparable study exists for manipulation-specific reward function
design.

\paragraph{Simulation infrastructure.}
ManiSkill3~\citep{maniskill3} provides GPU-parallelized simulation, a standardized
API across 18+ tabletop manipulation tasks, and built-in support for PPO baselines,
making it ideal for controlled reward geometry sweeps.

\paragraph{Hierarchical task reward design.}
DrS~\citep{zhuang2024drs} learns reusable dense reward functions for multi-stage tasks
by distilling sparse success signals into per-stage dense rewards. Like our work, it
studies stage-decomposed reward design; unlike ours, it focuses on reward
\emph{magnitude} learned from data, not the \emph{functional form} (curvature, support)
analyzed analytically. Hierarchical Potential-Based Reward Shaping (HPRS)~\citep{demir2024hprs}
extends Ng et al.'s potential-based framework to task specifications, but again studies
magnitude via potential functions rather than geometry.
Self-Adaptive Reward Shaping~\citep{selfadaptive2024} adapts reward weights online
but does not study the geometric properties of the shaping function itself.

\paragraph{Hierarchical reinforcement learning.}
Option-Critic~\citep{bacon2017option} and HIRO~\citep{nachum2018hiro} learn temporal
abstractions (options and sub-goals respectively) to decompose hierarchical tasks.
Our approach is complementary: task stages are \emph{given} by the task's
\texttt{evaluate()} function, so we do not need to learn when to switch stages.
We study how the reward geometry within each known stage affects learning.
HER~\citep{andrychowicz2017her} uses goal-conditioned relabeling to handle sparse
rewards; our work instead investigates how the dense shaping geometry interacts with
stage structure.

\paragraph{Curriculum learning.}
Narvekar et al.~\citep{narvekar2020curriculum} provide a framework for curriculum RL
in which training instances are ordered by difficulty. Our within-episode annealing
(\texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_episode}) is a special case of curriculum learning where the
geometry of the reward function itself changes within an episode. The stage-conditioned
extension (\texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_stage}) conditions the curriculum on task progress
rather than wall-clock time.
From Sparse to Dense~\citep{hao2025toddler} motivates sparse-to-dense reward
transitions from biological learning; our adaptive annealing formalizes this intuition
as a within-episode geometric schedule.

\paragraph{Concurrent work.}
Several 2026 preprints explore related but distinct directions: reward landscape
analysis for model-based planning, adaptive reward weighting, and two-stage curriculum
design. These address different algorithmic levers (planning objectives, weight
scheduling, task ordering) rather than the intrinsic geometry of the dense shaping
function itself.

\section{Reward Geometry Taxonomy}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

We decompose dense manipulation rewards into four independently controllable dimensions.
Each dimension is exposed as an environment kwarg (e.g.,
\texttt{reach\_variant="concave\_truncated"}), enabling factorial sweeps without
modifying the algorithm or task.

\subsection{Dimension 1: Reaching Shape}

The reaching component $r_d : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \to \mathbb{R}$ maps
end-effector-to-object distance $d$ to a scalar reward. We study eight static families
and three adaptive variants that vary in curvature, support, and monotonicity
(Table~\ref{tab:reach}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering

\toprule
\textbf{Name} & \textbf{Formula} & \textbf{Curvature} & \textbf{$\nabla r|_{d=0}$} \\

\texttt{tanh}              & $1 - \tanh(5d)$               & Convex & $-5$ \\
\texttt{linear}            & $\max(0, 1 - 5d)$             & Linear & $-5$ \\

\texttt{tanh\_sq}          & $1 - \tanh(25d^2)$            & Concave, no cutoff & $0$ \\

\end{tabular}

\subsection{Dimension 2: Stage Gating}

Many manipulation tasks are hierarchical: the robot must reach, then grasp, then place.
We study how the placement reward $r_p$ is conditioned on the grasping phase variable
$c_t \in \{0,1\}$ (or a continuous proxy):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{hard}: $r_p \cdot c_t$ \quad (binary mask; baseline)
    \item \texttt{soft}: $r_p \cdot \sigma(k(\tfrac{1}{2} - \text{openness}_t))$
    \quad (continuous gripper closure)
    \item \texttt{additive}: $r_p$ \quad (no gate; all stages always active)
    \item \texttt{curriculum}: hard gate for first 40\% of episode, then additive
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Dimension 3: Terminal Bonus}

At task success, we study three treatments of the terminal reward:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{hard\_jump}: override $r_t = r_{\max}$ at success (baseline)
    \item \texttt{smooth}: additive bonus $r_t \mathrel{+}= 0.5\, r_{\max} \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{success}]$
    \item \texttt{none}: pure shaping; no terminal override
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Tasks.}
We study four tasks spanning the main structural archetypes in tabletop manipulation:
PickCube-v1 (canonical pick-and-place, flat),
PushCube-v1 (pushing without grasping, flat),
LiftPegUpright-v1 (rotation-dominated, flat),
and StackCube-v1 (two-stage hierarchical).
PlaceSphere-v1 (three-stage hierarchical) is planned next.
A full listing of the 11 tasks with reward variant support is in
Appendix~\ref{app:tasks}.

\paragraph{Algorithm.}
All experiments use PPO~\citep{schulman2017ppo} via the ManiSkill3 fast GPU baseline
(256 parallel environments, 50-step rollouts).
Flat tasks use 5M total timesteps; StackCube-v1 uses 40M to allow the harder
hierarchical task sufficient training budget.
We plan to extend to SAC~\citep{haarnoja2018sac} and a model-based method
to assess algorithm-dependence of our findings.

\paragraph{Infrastructure.}
Reward variants are injected as environment kwargs, requiring no algorithm modification.
This is implemented in a dedicated \texttt{reward\_variants} module with three
independent axes.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
Each variant is evaluated with a \emph{deterministic} policy (mean action, no noise)
on 16 parallel environments every 25 training iterations.
Primary metric: \texttt{eval/success\_once}. Secondary metrics: eval return,
explained variance, gradient norm, value loss, and steps to 80\% success.
Train/eval success gap is tracked as a reward hacking diagnostic.

\paragraph{Compute.}
RTX 4070 laptop (8GB VRAM). At 256 envs and 5M steps, one run takes approximately
45--60 minutes. Multi-seed validation (3 seeds per configuration) is feasible within
the project timeline.

\section{Theory of Reward Geometry}
\label{sec:theory}

\subsection{From Static to Adaptive $k$: The Design Progression}

The experiments in Section~\ref{sec:results} trace a natural arc in the design of
reaching rewards. The concave truncated baseline has the form
\begin{equation}
    r_d(d) = \max\!\bigl(0,\, 1 - (k\,d)^2\bigr),
\end{equation}
with fixed $k$. This is a concave parabola that is exactly zero beyond $d = 1/k$: the
\emph{support radius} is $R = 1/k$. The core problem (F3) is that $R$ must simultaneously
be large enough to provide gradient signal from hard initialisations and small enough to
prevent a soft hover attractor near the goal. No single value of $k$ satisfies both.

The time-adaptive variants (\texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_episode}, \texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_stage})
address this by making $k$ a function of elapsed time within an episode, starting small
(wide $R$) and growing large (tight $R$). This resolves the tradeoff on flat tasks but
fails on hierarchical ones because \emph{time is a poor proxy for task progress}: by
mid-episode on StackCube, the time clock has already tightened $k$ while the robot is
still in stage~1 (F10).

The natural resolution is to let $k$ depend directly on the current distance $d$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{When far from the goal} ($d$ large): use small $k$ (wide $R$) to ensure
    the policy receives a gradient regardless of initialization.
    \item \textbf{When close to the goal} ($d$ small): use large $k$ (tight $R$) to
    create strong inward pull and suppress the soft hover attractor.
\end{itemize}
This gives a reward that is \emph{geometrically self-adapting}: the support radius
$R(d) = 1/k(d)$ automatically widens or narrows based on where the policy is, with
no internal state or time clock.

\subsection{The Distance-Adaptive Reward Function}

We parametrize $k(d)$ with an exponential interpolation between two extreme values:
\begin{equation}
    k(d) = k_{\min} + (k_{\max} - k_{\min})\,e^{-\alpha d},
    \label{eq:kd}
\end{equation}
so $k(0) = k_{\max}$ (tight near goal) and $k(d) \to k_{\min}$ as $d \to \infty$ (wide
far from goal). The full reward is
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{r(d) = \max\!\Bigl(0,\; 1 - \bigl(k(d)\cdot d\bigr)^2\Bigr),
    \quad k(d) = k_{\min} + (k_{\max} - k_{\min})\,e^{-\alpha d}.}
    \label{eq:acd}
\end{equation}
We use $k_{\min}=2$, $k_{\max}=18$, $\alpha=10$ in all experiments reported here;
these are the first configuration we tried and further tuning may yield better results.

\subsection{Geometry of the Reward: The Scaled Distance $\varphi(d) = k(d)\cdot d$}

Because $r(d) = \max(0, 1 - \varphi(d)^2)$, the shape of the reward is entirely
determined by the \emph{scaled distance} $\varphi(d)$. Understanding $r$ reduces to
understanding $\varphi$.

\paragraph{Outer cutoff.}
The reward reaches zero when $\varphi(d) = 1$. For large $d$, $k(d) \approx k_{\min}$,
so the outer cutoff occurs near $d = 1/k_{\min}$. With $k_{\min}=2$, this is $\approx 0.5$m:
numerically, $r(d) = 0$ for $d \gtrsim 0.46$m. This is the effective \emph{outer support radius}.

\paragraph{Inner basin.}
Near $d=0$, $k(d) \approx k_{\max} = 18$, so the reward gradient is steep ---
equal to the gradient of $\max(0, 1-(18d)^2)$, a tight parabola with effective radius
$1/k_{\max} \approx 0.056$m. The policy thus experiences a strong inward pull within
the last $\approx 6$cm of approach.

\paragraph{Does a moat exist?}
$\varphi'(d) = k(d) + k'(d)\,d = k_{\min} + (k_{\max}-k_{\min})\,e^{-\alpha d}(1 - \alpha d)$.
For $d > 1/\alpha$, the factor $(1-\alpha d)$ is negative, so $\varphi'$ can become
negative (i.e., $\varphi$ can have a local maximum). If $\varphi$ has a local maximum
$\varphi^*$, then:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If $\varphi^* > 1$: the reward passes through zero twice, creating a
    genuinely \emph{dead zone} (outer ring where $r=0$) sandwiching an outer lobe
    of nonzero reward.
    \item If $\varphi^* < 1$: the reward never reaches zero at the local max ---
    there is no dead zone, only a \emph{shallow moat} (local minimum) in the
    reward profile, followed by a small outer bump.
\end{itemize}
The worst case of $\varphi'$ occurs at $d^* = 2/\alpha$, where
$\varphi'(d^*) = k_{\min} - (k_{\max}-k_{\min})e^{-2}$.
Setting this to zero gives the moat-existence condition:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{k_{\max}}{k_{\min}} > 1 + e^{2} \approx 8.4.
    \label{eq:moat}
\end{equation}
With $k_{\min}=2$, $k_{\max}=18$: $k_{\max}/k_{\min} = 9 > 8.4$, so a moat
\emph{does} exist. Numerically, $\varphi$ has a local max at $d\approx 0.165$m
($\varphi \approx 0.837$) and a local min at $d\approx 0.245$m ($\varphi \approx 0.828$),
giving $r \approx 0.299$ at the moat and $r \approx 0.314$ at the outer bump. The moat
is very shallow (depth $\approx 0.015$) because $k_{\max}/k_{\min} = 9$ is only just above
the threshold $8.4$.

\subsection{Parameter Roles}

Each of the three parameters controls a distinct geometric property:

\paragraph{$k_{\min}$: outer support radius.}
$R_{\mathrm{outer}} \approx 1/k_{\min}$. Decreasing $k_{\min}$ widens the region where
the policy receives a nonzero gradient signal --- improving robustness to hard
initialisations. However, it also lowers the ratio $k_{\max}/k_{\min}$, which can
eliminate the moat (Eq.~\eqref{eq:moat}) and reduce the inner basin tightness.
With $k_{\min}=2$, $R_{\mathrm{outer}} \approx 0.5$m.

\paragraph{$k_{\max}$: inner basin tightness and moat depth.}
$R_{\mathrm{inner}} \approx 1/k_{\max}$ controls how tightly the gradient is
concentrated near the goal. Increasing $k_{\max}$ sharpens the inner basin but also
deepens the moat (increases $\varphi^*$). From Eq.~\eqref{eq:moat} and
$\Delta k = k_{\max} - k_{\min}$, the moat vanishes when $\Delta k \leq k_{\min}\,e^2$,
equivalently $k_{\max} \leq k_{\min}(1+e^2)$. For $k_{\min}=2$ this threshold is
$\Delta k \approx 14.8$ (i.e.\ $k_{\max} \approx 16.8$): with $\Delta k \leq 14$ the
reward is a smooth, strictly decreasing function; with $\Delta k \geq 15$ a moat
appears. Our configuration ($k_{\max}=18$, $\Delta k = 16$) sits just above the threshold,
producing a very shallow moat. With $k_{\max}=18$, $R_{\mathrm{inner}} \approx 0.056$m.

\paragraph{$\alpha$: transition rate and moat location.}
$\alpha$ controls how quickly $k(d)$ transitions from $k_{\max}$ (near goal) to
$k_{\min}$ (far away). The moat, when it exists, appears at $d \approx 1.65/\alpha$
(between $1/\alpha$ and $2/\alpha$). Crucially, $\alpha$ also determines whether the moat
is visible in the reward or hidden under the $r=0$ clamp: the moat is visible only
if $\varphi$ at the local maximum is less than 1. For the current $k_{\min}$, $k_{\max}$,
this visibility requires $\alpha$ to be large enough that the moat location is close
to the goal (small $d$), where $\varphi < 1$. With $\alpha=10$, the moat is at
$d\approx 0.165$m, well within the support radius (0.46m), and thus visible.
Lowering $\alpha$ to~5 would push the moat toward $d\approx 0.35$m, where
$\varphi > 1$ and the reward is already zero --- the moat becomes invisible.
Raising $\alpha$ to~15 moves the moat to $d\approx 0.11$m and deepens it to $r\approx 0.69$,
making it a more substantial feature of the landscape.

We have not yet varied these parameters experimentally; the configuration
$k_{\min}=2$, $k_{\max}=18$, $\alpha=10$ is the first one we tested.

\subsection{Why Distance-Adaptivity Resolves the Hierarchical Failure}

The failure of time-based annealing on StackCube (F10) stems from the episode clock
becoming decoupled from task progress at stage boundaries. Distance-based adaptivity
eliminates this coupling entirely: $k(d)$ is a \emph{pure function of the current
robot--object distance}, with no memory of time, stages, or episode history.

When a stage transition occurs in StackCube (grasp achieved; now reach for placement
target), $d$ jumps from near-zero (successful grasp) to a large value (distance to
placement location). This jump in $d$ causes $k(d)$ to drop back toward $k_{\min}$,
automatically widening the support radius for the new sub-goal --- exactly the behavior
that the stage-clock reset in \texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_stage} tried to engineer
explicitly. Here it happens for free, as a consequence of the geometry.

More generally, \texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance} is \emph{geometrically adaptive
but temporally static}: it is a closed-form function of $d$ alone, with no internal
state. The ``adaptivity'' lives in the reward landscape's curvature, not in any
schedule. Table~\ref{tab:design-arc} places this in the context of the full design
progression.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\caption{Arc of reward design decisions and their failure modes.}
\label{tab:design-arc}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Design} & \textbf{Mechanism} & \textbf{Failure mode} \\
\midrule
Static infinite (\texttt{tanh})            & Fixed wide $R$            & Soft hover attractor \\

\paragraph{F1 — Hover attractors cause initialisation-dependent failure.}
\texttt{tanh} and \texttt{tanh\_sq} reach 1.0 on easy seeds (42, 123) but
completely fail on seed~1 (Figure~\ref{fig:per_seed}, left panel).
Formally, let $d(s)$ be the distance to the goal and $r_d(d)$ be the dense reward. A state $s_h$ with $d_h = d(s_h) > 0$ acts as a \emph{hover attractor} if the expected gradient of the advantage function $A(s,a)$ pushes the policy toward maintaining $d_h$ rather than progressing toward $d=0$.
For infinite-support rewards like $r_d(d) = 1 - \tanh(kd)$, the gradient $\nabla_d r_d(d) = -k\text{sech}^2(kd)$ exists and is non-zero everywhere. This creates a continuous gradient ascent path toward a local region where the shaping reward is high, but crossing into the next task stage (e.g., grasping the object) requires a coordinated sequence of actions that temporarily lowers expected return. Thus, hard initialisations converge to this hover attractor ($d \approx 0.3$m) and never escape.
Explained variance remains $\approx 0.96$ throughout, confirming the failure is
policy-level, not a value function issue.

\paragraph{F2 — Finite support prevents hover attractors but causes mode-switching instability.}
\texttt{concave\_truncated} ($R{=}0.2$) survives seed~1 (peak 0.9375) but
oscillates dramatically and ends at 0.625 after 5M steps.
Mathematically, the abrupt zero at $R = 0.2$m in $r_d(d) = \max(0, 1 - (d/R)^2)$ creates a non-differentiable boundary in the state-reward mapping. When the policy $\pi_\theta$ is near this boundary, slight errors in the value function $V(s)$ cause the advantage estimate to swing wildly between positive and negative depending on whether a sampled trajectory crosses into the $d < R$ zone.
This discontinuity in the incentive boundary forces the policy to repeatedly cross it in both directions, producing \emph{mode-switching} (alternating between exploring outside the zone and exploiting inside it) rather than stable convergence.
Narrowing to $R{=}0.1$ slows initial learning $\approx\!2.5\times$ and produces
the same instability.

\paragraph{F3 — The robustness-ceiling tradeoff is fundamental.}
Wide support (large $R$): initialisation-robust, hover-prone.
Narrow support (small $R$): hover-resistant, mode-switching unstable.
No static geometry resolves both simultaneously.
This tradeoff is not captured by potential-based shaping theory~\citep{ng1999shaping},
which addresses asymptotic optimality but not training dynamics under finite samples.

\paragraph{F4 — Near-goal curvature does not independently predict performance.}
\texttt{tanh} (convex, $\nabla r|_{d=0} \neq 0$) and \texttt{tanh\_sq}
(concave, $\nabla r|_{d=0} = 0$) perform nearly identically across all seeds.
H2 (stage-handoff geometry) is not confirmed as an independent effect;
curvature matters only in conjunction with support structure.

\paragraph{F5 — Progress-gated support narrowing fails on hard seeds (chicken-and-egg).}
\texttt{adaptive\_concave\_threshold} ($R: 0.4 \to 0.1$, triggered when
\texttt{success} $\geq 0.5$) achieves 1.0 on seed~42 and 0.75 on seed~123,
but 0.0 on seed~1: the wide initial support $R{=}0.4$ creates an even stronger
hover attractor than $R{=}0.2$, so the threshold is never crossed and $R$ never
narrows.

\paragraph{F6 — Within-episode annealing resolves the tradeoff.}
\texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_episode} anneals the scale $k$ from 2 to 20 over the max episode length $T$. Formally, at step $t \in [0, T]$:
\begin{equation}
    r(d, t) = 1 - \tanh(k(t) \cdot d), \quad k(t) = 2 + 18 \cdot \frac{t}{T}
\end{equation}
Early in each episode ($t \approx 0$), $k(t)$ is small, resulting in a wide gradient footprint that bootstraps exploration regardless of overall training progress.
Late in each episode ($t \approx T$), $k(t)$ is large, concentrating the gradient strictly near the goal (the hover reward at $d=0.2$m shrinks from $1-\tanh(0.4) \approx 0.61$ to $1-\tanh(4.0) \approx 0.004$).
Because the curriculum is delivered within every episode, hard seeds get the wide
bootstrap phase on every reset---breaking the chicken-and-egg.
Results: mean $0.979 \pm 0.029$ versus next-best $0.646 \pm 0.457$ for \texttt{tanh\_sq}.

\paragraph{F10 --- Within-episode annealing catastrophically fails on hierarchical tasks.}
\texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_episode} achieves mean $0.021$ on StackCube---virtually
zero---despite being the best variant on all three flat tasks. This is a complete
ranking inversion.

The mechanism is the interaction between within-episode annealing and hierarchical
task structure. In PickCube, the task is effectively \emph{flat}: the robot reaches,
grasps, and places in one continuous motion, so wall-clock episode time $\approx$
task progress. In StackCube, the task has \emph{two committed contact stages}: grasp
the lower cube, then place it on the upper cube. The within-episode annealing
schedules $k$ as a function of \emph{time}, not \emph{task progress}. By the
mid-episode point when $k$ is already large, the robot may still be in stage~1
(reaching for the lower cube), and the now-tight reaching gradient provides
insufficient signal for a robot even slightly off-center. The schedule mismatch
prevents the policy from ever consolidating a reliable grasp, collapsing performance.

\paragraph{F11 --- Finite support enables hierarchical manipulation; time-based annealing does not.}
\texttt{concave\_truncated} achieves peak success $1.000$ on all three seeds
(Table~\ref{tab:stackcube}), confirming that finite support is sufficient for
hierarchical tasks. The zero-gradient zone at $d < R$ may actively \emph{help}: once
the robot is within reach, the reaching reward goes silent and stops competing with the
downstream grasping and placement signal, allowing the policy to commit to contact.
This is the inverse of the hover-attractor failure: reward \emph{silence} near the
goal creates a stable commitment region. However, \texttt{concave\_truncated} is slow
to learn: the first nonzero success does not appear until $\approx\!18$--$22$M steps
across seeds.

\paragraph{F12 --- Distance-adaptive reward matches peak performance with $\approx$2$\times$ faster convergence.}
\texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance} achieves peak \texttt{eval/success\_once} of
$1.000$ / $0.938$ / $1.000$ on seeds 1, 42, 123 (mean peak $\mathbf{0.979}$),
comparable to \texttt{concave\_truncated} (mean peak $1.000$), but with all three
seeds showing nonzero success by $\sim$7--12M steps versus $\sim$18--22M steps for
\texttt{concave\_truncated} --- roughly $2\times$ faster onset. The final values
(1.000 / 0.750 / 0.938) reflect late-training instability on seed~42, not a failure to
learn: seed~42 peaks at 0.938 at $\sim$20M steps before partially decaying.

The mechanism follows directly from Section~\ref{sec:theory}. Far from the goal
($d \approx 0.3$--$0.5$m), $k(d) \approx k_{\min} = 2$ and the outer support radius
$R \approx 0.5$m provides gradient signal even from hard initialisations. Close to the
goal ($d < 0.06$m), $k(d) \approx k_{\max} = 18$ tightens the basin, suppressing the
soft hover attractor. Neither regime is hardcoded to a time or stage: when a stage
transition occurs and $d$ jumps to a large value, the reward automatically widens back
to the exploration regime --- without any clock reset.

flat tasks is the immediate next experiment (Section~\ref{sec:planned}).

\section{Planned Experiments}
\label{sec:planned}

\subsection{Cross-Task Validation of \texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance}}

The most immediate open question is whether \texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance}
maintains the strong performance of \texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_episode} on flat tasks.
On a single-stage task, the distance $d$ decreases monotonically throughout the episode,
so the wide-to-tight transition of $k(d)$ mirrors the wide-to-tight annealing of
\texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_episode} --- but gated by actual proximity rather than time.
We predict no regression on PickCube, PushCube, or LiftPegUpright.

\textbf{Success criterion:} \texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance} achieves mean peak
$\geq 0.93$ on PickCube-v1 (matching \texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_episode}'s $0.979$
within tolerance) without any catastrophic failure seeds, and $\geq 0.93$ on PushCube-v1.
If confirmed, it becomes a \emph{universal} base reward: no task-type selection required.

\subsection{Parameter Sensitivity ($k_{\min}$, $k_{\max}$, $\alpha$)}

The current configuration ($k_{\min}=2$, $k_{\max}=18$, $\alpha=10$) is the first
we tested. Section~\ref{sec:theory} derives the moat-existence condition
$k_{\max}/k_{\min} > 1+e^2 \approx 8.4$ and characterises the role of each parameter.
Key ablations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Vary $k_{\max} \in \{14, 16, 18, 22\}$ to move across the moat threshold
    and assess whether the moat is beneficial, neutral, or harmful.
    \item Vary $k_{\min} \in \{1, 2, 3\}$ to change the outer support radius
    $R_{\mathrm{outer}} = 1/k_{\min} \in \{1.0, 0.5, 0.33\}$m.
    \item Vary $\alpha \in \{5, 10, 15\}$ to shift the moat location and transition sharpness.
\end{itemize}
These ablations will reveal which aspects of the geometry drive performance and
provide guidance for applying the reward to new tasks with different length scales.

\subsection{Deeper Hierarchies: PlaceSphere-v1}

PlaceSphere-v1 is a three-stage task (reach, grasp, place) with 13 reward components.
It is a stronger test of distance-adaptive rewards on hierarchical tasks because stage
transitions cause $d$ to jump \emph{twice}, each time returning $k(d)$ to the
wide-support regime. We plan 3 seeds comparing \texttt{tanh}, \texttt{concave\_truncated},
and \texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance}.

\subsection{Gating and Terminal Dimensions}

Fix \texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance} as the reaching variant and sweep the gating
and terminal bonus dimensions on PickCube-v1. Cross-validate top configurations on
StackCube-v1 and PlaceSphere-v1.

\subsection{Algorithm Generalization}

Repeat the top configurations with SAC and a model-based method to assess whether
the geometric findings are specific to on-policy PPO or more general.

\subsection{Sim-to-Real (Stretch)}

Validate the top configuration on the SO-101 robotic arm via the
\texttt{lerobot-sim2real} bridge supported in ManiSkill3.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{From task-type selection to a universal geometry.}
The initial playbook finding---flat tasks favour time-based annealing, hierarchical
tasks favour finite support---is real but incomplete. It describes a consequence of
a deeper principle: reward support should be wide when the agent is far from the goal
and tight when close. Time is a good proxy for distance on flat tasks (where $d$
decreases monotonically during a well-behaved episode) but a poor proxy on hierarchical
tasks (where $d$ resets at each stage). \texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance} removes
the proxy entirely by coupling the support radius directly to $d$, yielding a reward
that is self-adapting, stateless, and task-structure-agnostic in principle.

\textbf{The reward geometry perspective.}
Existing reward design literature focuses on magnitude (reward weights, normalisation)
or potential-based structure (shaping theorems). The present work argues that
\emph{geometry}---the shape of the reward as a function of distance---is an
independently important design axis. The hover attractor, the mode-switching instability,
the schedule-mismatch failure, and the faster convergence of
\texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance} are all consequences of reward geometry rather
than magnitude. Potential-based theory~\citep{ng1999shaping} is silent on all of them.

\textbf{Limitations and open questions.}
The parameter triple $(k_{\min}, k_{\max}, \alpha)$ introduces new hyperparameters.
We have only tested one configuration; Section~\ref{sec:planned} describes a systematic
ablation. The moat condition $k_{\max}/k_{\min} > 1+e^2$ is a precise analytical
criterion but whether the moat is beneficial, neutral, or harmful in practice is an
open empirical question. Finally, our experiments use PPO exclusively; off-policy
methods may interact differently with reward geometry because they do not replay the
same near-goal trajectories within a single update batch.

\textbf{Train/eval success gap as a reward quality diagnostic.}
Explained variance is uniformly $\approx 0.96$ across all variants---uninformative as
a discriminator. The train/eval success gap is more revealing: a large gap indicates
the policy is exploiting the dense reward without achieving task completion under the
deterministic evaluation policy. Figure~\ref{fig:train_eval_gap} tracks this for the
PickCube variants.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented the Reward Engineering Playbook: a systematic study of how the
geometry of dense reward functions---not their magnitude or potential structure---causally
affects manipulation learning. Our key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Hover attractors} are a systematic failure mode of infinite-support
    rewards, causing initialisation-dependent policy collapse on PickCube and PushCube.
    \item \textbf{The robustness-ceiling tradeoff} between finite- and infinite-support
    geometry is fundamental: no static support radius resolves both the blind-region
    failure and the hover attractor simultaneously.
    \item \textbf{Within-episode annealing} (\texttt{adaptive\_tanh\_episode}) resolves
    the tradeoff on flat tasks (mean peak $0.979$ on PickCube) but causes a complete
    ranking inversion on hierarchical ones (mean peak $0.063$ on StackCube), because
    the episode time clock decouples from task progress at stage boundaries.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance}} --- derived from the principle
    that support radius should track the agent's current distance --- achieves mean peak
    $0.979$ on StackCube with $\approx\!2\times$ faster convergence than the best
    task-specific static reward, with no time clock or stage information required.
\end{enumerate}

The theoretical framework in Section~\ref{sec:theory} provides closed-form conditions
characterising the reward's geometry as a function of $(k_{\min}, k_{\max}, \alpha)$,
connecting the empirical results to an analytical foundation. Cross-task validation of
\texttt{adaptive\_concave\_distance} on flat tasks and a parameter sensitivity study
are the immediate next steps toward a fully unified, theoretically principled reward
function for robot manipulation.
